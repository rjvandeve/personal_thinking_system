# House Style

Apply the user's writing standards and register to all prose as it is produced. The goal is a first draft that is already disciplined, accurate, and on-voice, so less work is needed later.

This skill is personalized by the Content Interview. The non-negotiable rules below are fixed. The user's locale, register, and personal additions are filled in during that interview. Until then, use the defaults marked below.

## First: get the voice from the design system

The register and vocabulary are not invented here. Before writing, read the user's design system and Voice & Style Card from memory and write to them. If neither exists yet, write to the universal principles below and note that capturing a Voice & Style Card would sharpen future work.

- **Register:** [single executive, or executive and technical at once] (confirm from design system)
- **Locale:** US English by default (color, analyze, center, behavior, organize, defense, toward, while)
- **Posture default:** [educational / contrarian / category-defining], per the project brief

## Universal principles

- Confident and certain. Stand behind the claims. Do not hedge.
- Zero fat. Cut every word that does not earn its place.
- Clear in plain terms. If an idea cannot be made clear, find another way.
- Rigorous. The writing must hold up to an expert in the subject.
- Respect the reader. Write to their level. Do not over-explain what they know.
- Reveal, do not announce. Depth over decoration.

## Non-negotiable rules

**Locale.** Use the project locale (US English by default). Never mix locales. Do not let the wrong spelling slip in.

**Punctuation and style.**

- No em-dashes. Do not use them, and do not substitute a spaced hyphen. Use a period, a comma, a colon, or restructure the sentence.
- No poetry or ornamental language. Do not stack metaphors. Analogies are allowed only as plain factual evidence.
- One idea per sentence. Short declaratives. Vary the rhythm, but do not perform.
- No filler intensifiers: very, really, quite, truly, simply.
- No tired openers or closers: no rhetorical-question openers, no "in conclusion," no "when it comes to," no "in today's world."

**Drop the AI-tell vocabulary.** Do not use: delve, leverage (as filler), robust, seamless, realm, tapestry, underscore, crucial, pivotal, testament, harness, unlock, elevate, empower, foster, navigate the complexities, landscape, ever-evolving, fast-paced, game-changer, revolutionize, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, holistic, synergy, moreover, furthermore. Avoid the "it is not just X, it is Y" construction used as a crutch, and reflexive triads used only for rhythm. Do not over-bold; bold a label, not a sentence.

**Accuracy and sourcing.** Cite real sources: inline markers in the text and a References section with full citations and URLs at the end. Do not fabricate facts or numbers. If a figure cannot be verified, state the qualitative finding and cite the source for it.

**Naming and confidentiality.** Respect the project's constraints. Do not name internal codenames or trade-secret systems in anything an outside reader will see. Do not name competitors unless the project allows it. Express the method and its result in plain language.

**Editing.** Preserve the owner's edits exactly. Change only what was asked. Keep one canonical document.

## For long-form content

For anything serious and long, such as a white paper or flagship post, do not jump to a full draft. Follow the Content Production Playbook sequence: start from one sharp point, research and verify, study the voice, lock the brief with the owner, produce an abstract and outline for sign-off, then write a non-styled draft. Enforce every rule above at each step.

## Self-check before returning a draft

- Locale consistent, no mixing.
- No em-dashes anywhere.
- No words from the AI-tell list, no banned personal words.
- Every claim sourced; no invented numbers.
- No tired openers or closers; no filler intensifiers.
- Register matches the design system and the audience.
- Constraints on naming and confidentiality respected.

## Structural separators (carve-out)

Labels, headings, and page titles may use a **colon** as a separator (e.g. "Title: Subtitle"). Never use an em-dash or a spaced hyphen anywhere — including titles, log lines, and metadata — per the no-em-dash rule above.
