# Helper — Your Working Style

*The four habits that make building safe, made concrete. Read this before you build anything. These aren't bureaucracy — they're the difference between a tool that comes together cleanly and a tangle that has to be thrown away.*

---

## Why a non-technical person needs a "method"

You might assume the assistant handles all the discipline. It doesn't — not on its own. Left to go fast, an assistant will happily make five changes at once, claim things work without checking, and chase every new idea down a rabbit hole. The result is a mess that's hard to untangle, even for an expert.

The method below is how you stay in control. It's borrowed directly from how careful professional teams build — distilled to four habits.

---

## Habit 1 — One change at a time

**Build one small piece, finish it, prove it, then move on.**

The fastest way into trouble is asking for several things at once ("add this, fix that, and also change this"). When something then breaks, you can't tell which change caused it, and the fixes start causing new problems. This is called *compounding* — small messes multiplying into big ones.

**In practice:** insist on one piece at a time. If the assistant proposes doing several things, say: *"One at a time. Let's do just the first, finish it, and prove it works before the next."*

## Habit 2 — Plan before you build

**Decide what and how before any building starts.**

Thinking is cheap and easy to change. Half-built work is expensive and painful to change. So the order is always: understand the goal → make a plan → review the plan → *then* build.

**In practice:** before building, ask: *"Make a plan for this before you build anything. Tell me what you'll do, in what order, and what could go wrong. I'll review it first."* Read the plan. If it's bigger than a quick job, have it written down so you can look it over with your coach.

## Habit 3 — Prove it works

**Every piece gets demonstrated before it counts as done.**

"It should work" is the most dangerous phrase in building. Done means *shown to work*, not *probably fine*.

**In practice:** after each piece, ask: *"Prove it works. Show me exactly how you checked, and let me see the result."* If it can't be demonstrated, it isn't done — keep going.

A useful four-step version when something is broken:
1. **Investigate** — find the actual cause, don't guess.
2. **Prove me wrong** — challenge the first assumption about what's wrong.
3. **Explain the fix** — describe how you'll fix it before doing it.
4. **Prove it's fixed** — demonstrate the problem is actually gone.

## Habit 4 — Capture, don't chase

**New ideas mid-build get written down — not acted on immediately.**

You *will* think of new things while building. The temptation is to "just quickly add it." Don't. That's how the one-change-at-a-time habit dies and the mess begins.

**In practice:** when a new idea appears, say: *"Capture this as a task for later — don't do it now. Here's the idea: [describe]. Then let's get back to what we were doing."* The idea is safe (it's written down as a task), and your current work stays clean.

---

## The self-check before calling anything "done"

Borrowed from careful builders. Before a piece counts as finished, ask:

- Did this do exactly **one** thing?
- Is the new idea I had **captured as a task** (not half-built into this)?
- Did I avoid touching anything unrelated?
- Can I actually **prove it works**?
- Would someone reviewing this see **only what was necessary**?

---

## A word on going slow

This will feel slower than "just build it all." It isn't — it's faster, because you almost never have to throw work away or untangle a mess. Steady, proven, one-piece-at-a-time building is the quickest path to a tool that actually works. Your coach will keep you honest on this; it's the habit people are most tempted to abandon.
