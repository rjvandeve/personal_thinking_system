# Helper — Guardrails & Safety

*What keeps building safe, so you can be confident nothing breaks irreversibly. Read this so you understand the safety net under you — and know what to do if something feels wrong.*

---

## The core promise: everything is reversible

The single most reassuring fact about building this way: **nothing is permanent.** With the version-history safety net set up (your coach does this first), every proven piece of work is saved as a numbered version. If a change makes things worse, you go back to the last good version. You can't paint yourself into a corner you can't escape.

This is why the method insists on *proving each piece before saving it* — so the saved versions are always known-good points you can safely return to.

## The five guardrails

**1. You approve each step.**
The assistant proposes; you approve. Nothing significant happens without your say-so. If it ever starts doing several things you didn't approve, stop it: *"Stop — let's go one step at a time, and show me before you change anything."*

**2. One change at a time.**
A single small change is easy to check and easy to undo. Five tangled changes are neither. This is your biggest protection against messes. (See "Your Working Style.")

**3. Prove before you trust.**
Never accept "it should work." A piece isn't done until it's demonstrated. Unproven work is where problems hide.

**4. Capture new ideas instead of chasing them.**
Mid-build detours are how things break. New ideas become written-down tasks, not impulsive changes.

**5. Keep the stakes low — especially at first.**
Build personal tools and practice on things that don't matter if they're imperfect. Don't point your first builds at anything important or irreversible in your real work.

## If something feels wrong

Trust the feeling and slow down. A calm, plain-language script:

> Something doesn't seem right. Stop making changes. Tell me, in plain language: what's the current state, what was the last thing that worked, and what are our options to get back to a good state?

Then bring your coach in. Going *back* to a known-good version is always available — there's no situation where the only path is forward.

## What NOT to do

- **Don't let it rush you.** Speed is the enemy of safety here. Slower-and-proven beats faster-and-broken every time.
- **Don't approve things you don't understand.** Ask for a plain-language explanation first: *"Explain what this does and why, like I'm not technical."*
- **Don't point early builds at anything important.** Practice on the low-stakes stuff until the method is second nature.
- **Don't skip proving it works** just because the draft looks fine — "looks fine" is exactly what proving-it-works is there to catch.

## The reassurance to hold onto

You are working with a safety net (versions you can return to), a method (one proven step at a time), and a coach (for anything that feels uncertain). Within those three, there's very little that can go wrong that can't be undone. That's the whole point of building this way: it makes building *safe enough for a careful non-expert* to do confidently.
