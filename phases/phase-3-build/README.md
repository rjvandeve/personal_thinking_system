# Phase 3 — Build Small Tools Safely

*Goal: build small, personal tools that solve your own workflow problems — directing the work in plain language, with a method that keeps everything safe and reversible. This is the most hands-on phase, and the one where your coach walks beside you the whole way.*

---

## Read this first: a different kind of phase

Phases 1 and 2 you can largely run yourself. **Phase 3 is different.** Building software — even small, personal software — has more moving parts, and the cost of a careless step is higher. So this phase is **heavily coached**: your coach walks you through building your first tool step by step, live. These guides are the map; your coach is the guide.

The good news: you still don't write any code. You *describe* what you want, *approve* each step, and *verify* the result. The assistant builds. Your job is judgment and direction — exactly the part a human should keep.

By the end, you'll have built a real, working tool, and — more importantly — you'll understand the *method* that makes building safe, so the second tool is easier than the first.

---

## What you'll be able to do

- Turn "I wish I had a tool that…" into a working tool, without writing code.
- Direct the build in plain language and verify each step actually works.
- Do it **safely** — nothing breaks irreversibly, every step is reversible, and you approve the work as it goes.

## Which runner you're using

This phase works with two kinds of assistant. Wherever setup differs, the helper guides say so explicitly.

- **Claude Cowork** — the desktop assistant. Your tool's files live in a folder on your machine that you connect to the session.
- **Hermes** — a self-hosted runner. Your tool's files live in a workspace it can read and write, and it can become the "front door" you message to drive a build.

Both end up at the same place: a working tool, built one proven piece at a time. Pick whichever you have; the day-to-day asks are identical.

## What you'll set up (your coach does this with you — in order)

This phase introduces one new tool, and your coach sets it up live:

1. **A version history (a "safety net")** — think of it as a history book for your project. Every change is saved as a numbered version, so you can always see what changed and undo anything. It also holds your **list of ideas and tasks**, so nothing gets lost — the "capture, don't chase" habit, made real. (On most setups this is a free GitHub account; your coach handles the wiring.)

You won't set this up alone, and you won't set it up all at once. Comfort first, then the next layer. The "front door" — driving a build by simply messaging your assistant — is introduced *after* the basics feel natural, not on day one.

---

## The method is the point

The single most important thing in this phase isn't any tool — it's the **method**. It's the same discipline professional builders use, and it's just four core habits applied to building:

1. **One change at a time.** Build one small piece, finish it, prove it, then move to the next. Never five things at once.
2. **Plan before you build.** Decide *what* and *how* before any building starts. Thinking is cheap; tangled half-built work is expensive.
3. **Prove it works.** Every piece gets demonstrated before it counts as done. "It should work" is not "it works."
4. **Capture, don't chase.** New ideas mid-build get written down as tasks — not acted on immediately. The current piece gets finished first.

Internalize these and you can build safely. Ignore them and even the assistant can make a mess. The guides below are all built on these four.

## Your first build (entirely with your coach)

1. Your coach helps you set up the version-history safety net.
2. You pick something small and real — a genuine but low-stakes workflow annoyance.
3. Together you **describe** it, then have the assistant **plan** it.
4. You break the plan into small pieces and build them **one at a time**, proving each works.
5. You end with a working tool — and the method in your hands.

The "front door" workflow and bigger builds come later, once the first one feels good.

---

## What "good" looks like

- You built something real and it works.
- You can explain the method — one piece at a time, plan first, prove it works, capture ideas.
- You feel in control of the process, not at the mercy of it.

This is the top of the build arc. With it in place, your assistant remembers your world, sounds and looks like you, and can build you tools — and you know how to direct all of it.
