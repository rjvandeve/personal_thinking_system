# Helper — From Idea to Working Tool

*The step-by-step flow you and your coach follow to turn "I wish I had a tool that…" into a real, working tool. Each step has a plain-language prompt. You direct and approve; the assistant builds.*

---

## The shape of a build

```
Describe  →  Plan  →  Break into small pieces  →  Build one piece  →  Prove it  →  (repeat)  →  Done
```

You'll loop the middle steps — one small piece at a time — until the tool is complete. That loop *is* the method from "Your Working Style."

---

## Step 1 — Describe what you want

Don't describe a solution — describe the *problem* and what "done" looks like.

> I want to build a small tool for myself. Here's the problem: [describe the annoyance in plain words]. Here's what I wish I could do instead: [the outcome you want]. Don't build anything yet — ask me questions until you understand it well enough to plan.

Let it ask questions. Good answers here save a lot of rework.

## Step 2 — Get a plan (before any building)

> Now make a plan. Tell me, in plain language: what we'll build, the main pieces, what order to build them in, and anything that could go wrong or that you're unsure about. Don't build yet — I want to review the plan with my coach first.

Review it together. If it's sizable, have it written to a file so you can read it properly:

> Write this plan to a file called `plan-[tool-name].md` so I can review it before we start.

## Step 3 — Break it into small pieces

> Break this plan into the smallest sensible pieces. Each piece should be one small, finishable, provable step. For each, tell me what it does and how we'll know it works. List them in order.

This gives you a checklist of tiny, safe steps instead of one scary leap.

## Step 4 — Build ONE piece

> Let's build only the first piece: [name it]. Just that one. When it's done, stop and show me.

Resist the urge to say "and also do the next one." One at a time.

## Step 5 — Prove that piece works

> Prove this piece works. Show me how you checked and let me see the result.

If it works → save the progress (your coach shows you how this gets recorded as a numbered version, so it's never lost). If it doesn't → use the investigate-and-fix loop from "Your Working Style," then re-prove.

## Step 6 — Repeat, one piece at a time

Go back to Step 4 for the next piece. Each loop: build one, prove it, save it. The tool grows safely, piece by piece, and at every point you have something that works.

## When a new idea strikes mid-build

> Capture this as a task for later — don't build it now: [the idea]. Then let's get back to the piece we were on.

---

## Where the safety net and the "front door" fit

- **The version-history safety net** (set up first, with your coach) quietly does two jobs during this flow: it **saves each proven piece as a numbered version** — your safety net, so anything can be undone — and it **holds your captured ideas and tasks** so the "capture, don't chase" habit has a real home.
- **The "front door"** (introduced later) is the point where you stop working through technical tools and just **message your assistant** what you want, and it runs the build behind the scenes. You'll graduate to it once the basic flow feels natural — your coach will know when you're ready. On Cowork this is the desktop chat; on Hermes it's the message channel you've wired to your runner.

---

## The mindset for your first build

Pick something genuinely small and genuinely yours — a real annoyance, but one where an imperfect first attempt costs nothing. The goal of the first build isn't the tool; it's the *method*. Once the loop feels natural, the tools get more ambitious and the method stays exactly the same.
