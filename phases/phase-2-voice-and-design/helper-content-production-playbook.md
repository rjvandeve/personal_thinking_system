# Helper — The Content Production Playbook

*The process and the standards for producing serious written content: white papers, long-form posts, articles, and any piece that has to hold up. It works for any project. The universal rules live here; the specific voice comes from your design system.*

> Two skills do the heavy lifting around this playbook. `house-style` enforces these writing standards automatically as you draft. `jobs-ive-taste` critiques the result before it ships. This playbook is the method that ties them together. Both are installed during Phase 2 setup.

---

## The one idea to hold onto

The writing standards in this playbook are non-negotiable and the same for everyone. The voice for a given piece is not invented here. It comes from your design system (the manifesto, the taste page, the Voice & Style Card). Standards are universal; register and vocabulary are yours. That separation is what keeps your content both disciplined and distinctly you.

---

## Required inputs (collect before starting)

The first is mandatory; the rest make the piece sharp.

- **Design system** *(required)* — your Figma design system, which defines voice, tone, and visual language. If you don't have one yet, build it first (see the Design Interview). The standards below are universal; the register and vocabulary come from here.
- **The point** — one or two sentences stating the single, ownable claim the piece will make.
- **Audience** — who reads it, and what they should understand or do.
- **Locale** — the spelling and usage standard (default US English unless specified).
- **Constraints** — naming, confidentiality, or competitive rules (codenames that must not appear, competitors that must not be named).
- **Evidence sources** — where facts and figures come from, and whether quotes are allowed.
- **Output and location** — the format for review and where the working document lives.

---

## The process (idea to reviewable draft)

1. **Start from one sharp point.** Begin with a single, ownable claim in one or two sentences before anything else is written. A contrarian or category-defining view beats a summary. If the point isn't clear, stop and find it.
2. **Research and verify.** Gather evidence before writing. Use web research for facts and citable sources; read the foundational material, including your design system. Verify every claim, prefer primary sources, and never invent numbers. If a figure can't be verified, state the qualitative finding and cite it.
3. **Study the voice in the design system first.** Before writing a word, read the voice material in your design system. Pull the register and rules from there, then write to them.
4. **Interview the owner (you).** Lock the key decisions before drafting: audience, positioning and posture, scope and length, structure, evidence, naming and confidentiality, locale, output format. The assistant should ask these as quick multiple-choice questions. Don't start a large draft without the answers.
5. **Abstract and outline for feedback.** Produce a tight abstract and a section-by-section outline first. Get sign-off on the thesis, structure, and evidence before the full draft.
6. **Draft as a non-styled document for review.** Write the full draft plain, with no design styling. Styling is a separate later pass. Keep one canonical working document; avoid version sprawl.
7. **Address comments and edits.** Resolve each comment. Preserve your own edits exactly. Change only what was asked.

## Voice and tone (universal principles)

The specific register and vocabulary come from your design system. These principles apply on top, every time:

- **Confident and certain.** Stand behind the claims. Don't hedge.
- **Zero fat.** Cut every word that doesn't earn its place.
- **Clear in plain terms.** If an idea can't be made clear, find another way.
- **Rigorous.** It must hold up to an expert in the subject. No hand-waving.
- **Match the register** the design system and audience call for (sometimes one executive register; sometimes executive and technical at once).
- **Respect the reader.** Write to their level. Don't over-explain what they already know.
- **Reveal, don't announce.** Depth over decoration.

## The non-negotiable writing rules

*(These are enforced automatically by the `house-style` skill. They're here so you know what it's doing.)*

**Language and locale.** Set the locale as a project input; default US English (color, analyze, center, behavior, organize, defense, toward, while). Never mix locales.

**Punctuation and style.**

- **No em-dashes.** Don't use them, and don't substitute a spaced hyphen. Use a period, comma, colon, or restructure.
- **No poetry or ornamental language.** Don't stack metaphors. Analogies only as plain factual evidence.
- **One idea per sentence.** Short declaratives; vary the rhythm but don't perform.
- **No filler intensifiers** (very, really, quite, truly, simply).
- **No tired openers or closers** (no rhetorical-question openers, no "in conclusion," "when it comes to," "in today's world").

**Drop the AI-tell vocabulary.** These signal generic AI writing, so don't use them: *delve, leverage (as filler), robust, seamless, realm, tapestry, underscore, crucial, pivotal, testament, harness, unlock, elevate, empower, foster, navigate the complexities, landscape, ever-evolving, fast-paced, game-changer, revolutionize, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, holistic, synergy, moreover, furthermore.* Avoid the "it is not just X, it is Y" crutch and reflexive triads used only for rhythm. Don't over-bold; bold a label, not a sentence.

**Accuracy and sourcing.** Cite real sources: inline markers plus a References section with full citations and URLs. Don't fabricate facts or numbers.

**Naming and confidentiality.** Respect project constraints. Don't expose internal codenames or trade-secret systems to outside readers; don't name competitors unless allowed. Express the method and result in plain language.

**Editing.** Preserve the owner's edits. Change only what was asked. Keep one canonical document.

---

## Deliverable conventions

- A **non-styled draft** for review; design is applied in a later pass.
- Plain headings, bullets, and tables are fine. Inline citations and a References section are expected.
- Deliver in the shared drive or as a Word file, per your preference.
- **One canonical working document.** Trash superseded drafts.
