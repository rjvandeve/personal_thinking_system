# Design Interview

*The centerpiece of Phase 2's design track. You don't build a design system. You let the assistant interview you, study materials you already have, and build one for you. This is the script the Coach runs, plus the full question bank so you can see what's coming.*

---

## What's about to happen

In one focused session (20 to 40 minutes), the assistant will:

1. **Interview you** — a guided conversation about what you stand for and how you want to come across. You just answer.
2. **Study your existing materials** — your decks, PDFs, website. It pulls your real colors, fonts, tone, and feel out of them.
3. **Synthesize a design system** — it writes up your Brand Manifesto, Taste rules, and Foundations (following the [Design System Blueprint](../templates/design-system-blueprint.md)).
4. **Build it into Figma** — with your Coach, it renders the system as real Figma pages you can see and use.

You will not design anything. You answer questions and approve choices.

---

## Before you start: gather your "raw brand"

Spend ten minutes collecting things you already have. The more real material, the less guessing:

- 2 to 3 **slide decks** you've made (especially ones you liked)
- 1 to 2 **PDFs or documents** that represent you well
- Your **website** link (or your organization's)
- Your **logo**, if you have one (any format)
- Optional: a few **images or examples** from anywhere that make you think "yes, that feeling"

Put these somewhere the assistant can read them (your connected folder on Cowork, or the workspace on Hermes), or be ready to share the links.

---

## Step 1 — Run the interview

The Coach runs the question bank below as a real conversation, one or two questions at a time, following up on interesting answers and offering concrete options to react to. If you're running it yourself, paste this and then just talk:

> **Let's build my design system. Interview me first.** Act as a brand strategist and design director. Interview me to capture my visual identity and the soul of how I want to come across, the way a top studio would before designing anything. Work through the question bank in this file, but make it a real conversation: ask one or two at a time, follow up, and don't move on until you've got something specific. If I give a vague answer, push me gently for a concrete one. I am not a designer, so keep your questions in plain language and give me examples to react to when it helps. When we're done, summarize back to me what you heard: my manifesto, my aesthetic in a handful of adjectives, who I'm for, and what I'd never want to look or sound like. Don't build anything yet.

**How to answer:** be decisive and specific. "I want to feel trustworthy and modern, never flashy" beats "professional." When you don't know, say "you choose, and tell me why" and let it propose and react.

---

## The question bank

*Only a human can answer Part A. Parts B through D can be drawn partly from your existing materials, with you confirming.*

### Part A — The soul (only you can answer these)

**Purpose and belief**

- In one sentence, what do you (or your organization) actually do?
- What do you believe that others in your space don't? What's your "unpopular but right" view?
- If someone remembered only one thing about you, what should it be?

**Tenets**

- What are the 3 to 5 principles you'd never compromise on?
- What's a thing competitors do that you would refuse to do?

**Audience**

- Who is this for? Describe one real person: their role, what they care about, what they're tired of.
- Are you speaking to experts, to executives, to newcomers, or two of these at once?

**Feeling**

- When someone encounters your work, how should they feel? (Pick a few: confident, calm, energized, reassured, impressed, understood…)
- What feeling do you want to avoid at all costs? (e.g., generic, salesy, cold, chaotic)

### Part B — Voice (how you write)

- Formal or conversational? Where on that line?
- Do you use "we," "I," or neither?
- Long, considered sentences or short, punchy ones?
- Words or phrases you love and use often?
- Words, clichés, or buzzwords you'd never be caught using?
- Show me 2 to 3 things you've written that sound like you. *(Provide these; they're worth more than any answer.)*

### Part C — Aesthetic (how you look)

- Give me 5 to 8 adjectives for how it should look and feel. *(e.g., precise, warm, minimal, bold, classic, electric, restrained.)*
- Light or dark as the base?
- Busy and rich, or spare and calm?
- Sharp and geometric, or soft and rounded?
- A brand or product whose look you admire, and what specifically about it?
- A look you find overused or that you actively dislike?

### Part D — Existing reality (from your materials)

*The assistant extracts these from what you provide, then confirms with you:*

- Actual colors in current use (and which are "real" vs. accidental)
- Fonts currently used
- Logo or mark, if any
- Recurring layouts and image styles
- What's consistent across materials vs. what's all over the place

### How the assistant should use these

- Ask one or two at a time; follow the interesting threads.
- For aesthetic questions, offer concrete options to react to ("Does it feel more like A or B?"), since reacting is easier than inventing.
- Push past vague answers ("professional," "clean") to specifics ("trustworthy like a private bank, not a startup").
- At the end, play it back: a draft manifesto, the aesthetic adjectives, the audience, and a clear "never" list. Confirm before building.

## Step 2 — Feed it your materials

Once the interview feels complete:

> Now study my existing materials. I'm giving you [my decks / PDFs / website / logo]. Pull out everything real: the actual colors I use, the fonts, the tone of my writing, the kinds of images and layouts I gravitate to. Tell me what's consistent, what's inconsistent, and what you'd keep vs. fix. Where my materials and my interview answers disagree, flag it and ask me which wins.

Then attach the files or paste the links. This grounds the system in reality instead of aspiration.

## Step 3 — Have it write the design system

> Now synthesize everything, the interview and my materials, into a written design system following the structure in [`templates/design-system-blueprint.md`](../templates/design-system-blueprint.md). Draft Part 1 (the soul: Brand Manifesto, Taste & Composition, Creative Brief) and Part 2 (the foundations: colors, typography, spacing). Write the manifesto in my voice. Present it to me section by section so I can react and refine before anything goes into Figma. Also save a short Voice & Style Card to my memory so all my future content uses it.

You'll go back and forth here. Refine until it feels like you. This written system is valuable on its own, even before Figma.

## Step 4 — Build it into Figma (with your Coach)

This is the step your Coach sets up live, because it uses the Figma connection.

> Let's build this into Figma now. Create the design system file following the Blueprint: a page for the Manifesto, one for Taste & Composition, then the Foundations (color, type, spacing). Use the exact colors and fonts we agreed on. Build one page at a time, show me a screenshot of each, and we'll refine before moving on.

The assistant builds; you look at each page and react. When something's off, you say so in plain words ("too dark," "the headline font feels stiff") and it adjusts.

## Step 5 — Add the templates you'll actually use

Once Foundations look right, point it at the payoff (Part 4 of the Blueprint):

> Now build my reusable templates: a letterhead or memo, a slide template, and a one-pager, all using the design system we just built. These are the things I'll use constantly.

---

## A note on getting it right

- **Don't aim for perfect in one sitting.** A design system is refined over a few sessions. Get the soul and foundations solid; add components and templates as real needs come up.
- **React honestly.** "I don't like that" is useful. The assistant can't read your taste; your reactions are the design process.
- **Run the taste pass.** Before you call any page or template done, run it through [`phases/phase-2-voice-and-design/helper-jobs-ive-taste-pass.md`](../phases/phase-2-voice-and-design/helper-jobs-ive-taste-pass.md). That's what turns "fine" into "unmistakably good."

When you have a manifesto, a taste page, foundations, and a couple of templates you're proud of, you have a design system. Most professionals don't have one this clear.
